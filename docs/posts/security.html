Internet Security for Friends and Family

May 9, 2024

Note: This is intended for sharing with non-technical individuals. I am not a security expert by any means. But since I work in tech, I often find myself advising friends and family on some basics that they can do to be safer (or keep their parents safer) online. What follows is a very basic list of best practices that will significantly improve baseline security.

the short version

Stop reusing passwords between accounts and use a password manager (like 1Password) to generate and store long and unique passwords for each account. Then, turn on two factor authentication (2FA) anywhere that you can. That’s it.

Keep reading for the longer version.

Internet security is hard:

  • it is technical
  • malicious tactics are clever
  • it is unintuitive

But by changing a few key practices, you can signficantly improve your security on the internet to safeguard your identity and finances.

Understanding Internet Security

  • you are not the target; you are a target: the internet is a vast ocean and you’re just one fish among many. There are “nets” — scams, hacks, viruses, etc. — that are everywhere, trying to catch anyone not practicing basic internet security. The goal isn’t to become invincible but rather to become a less appealing target by enhancing your security habits.
  • breaches are common: visit haveibeenpwned.com to see if your email has been compromised in a breach. If it has (which is likely), don’t worry — that’s an indicator of your internet usage but not a reflection of your personal security practices. Breaches don’t happen because you are insecure, they happen because the internet is insecure.
  • the reality of hacking: Contrary to popular belief and dramatized portrayals, hacking often involves exploiting the simplest security lapses — like reusing passwords. A common scenario involves a hacker accessing a database of user data (including email and passwords) from a less secure site (think of an old forum where you once registered) and trying those email-password combinations on more critical sites, such as your email provider where you might reuse the same password.

This all might sound scary — but it’s actually good news, because this a really easy problem to solve by following just a few basic internet security practices!

Basic Internet Security Practices

Use strong and unique passwords combined with two factor authentication (2FA). That’s it. Doing so will make you significantly more secure on the internet.

What makes a password strong?

  • length (> 16 characters, ideally longer)

  • complexity

  • impersonal (no dates, initials, names, nicknames, etc)

But the greatest of these is length:

  • uP4d*83 <– cracked in minutes
  • ball coffee charter kidnap outlet <– cracked in centuries

Unique Passwords

Do NOT reuse passwords for accounts connected to:

  • email: email is the key to the castle since someone can reset nearly all other passwords if they have access to your email
  • banking: bank, paypal, taxes, investments, venmo, etc - this is your money, protect it!
  • retail: Amazon, online shopping, or anywhere where you’ve submitted and stored credit card info
  • social media: Facebook, Instagram, Twitter, LinkedIn, etc - these may seem less important, but if they’re hacked they can be used for social engineering to get access to more important accounts or for trying to scam people that trust you

Two Factor Authentication (2FA)

In addition to a password, you can add a second factor to better prove your identity when logging in. Examples, ordered from best to worst:

  • hardware security key, like yubikey (best, but most technical)
  • phone key (not SMS)
  • authenticator code
  • phone SMS code
  • email code (worst)

Configure two factor authentication on all important accounts where available and be very weary of accounts where two factor is not available.

Passwords Managers

A good password manager solves two of the three baseline practices: strong and unique passwords.

  • it lives in your browser and on your phone
  • it can generate complex passwords that are unique per website
  • it fills passwords in the browser for you
  • BUT it is the key to the castle so make sure this is very secure

I recommend 1Password, but a serious and entirely valid alternative: pen & paper (just make sure passwords are long and unique).

Additional Practices

  • update your devices and browser regularly so that security updates are installed.
  • use a strong password / pin on your computer and phone
