Internet Security for Friends and Family

August 27, 2025

Note: I wrote this for non-technical friends and family. I am not a security expert by any means but I often find myself advising folks on baseline practices that will keep you singificantly safer online.

the short version

  1. Don’t reuse passwords between accounts
  2. Use a password manager (like 1password) to generate and store long and unique passwords for each account.
  3. Turn on two factor authentication (2FA) anywhere that you can.

That’s it. Keep reading for the longer version.

Most “hacks” of your email or social media accounts are due to individuals reusing the same password again and again and again between sites.

Go visit haveibeenpwned.com to see if your email has been included in a data breach. Note that if it has (which is very likely), it has nothing to do with your own security practices; those breaches are due to companies having poor security practices that allows hackers to access internal systems.

These data breaches often include personally identifiable information (name, address, phone number, etc) but sometimes also include passwords. So what happens if you reuse the same password everywhere and it was included in a breach?

Now that password can be attempted against other accounts: email, paypal, facebook, etc. And without taking extra steps, it’s not trivial for someone to get into these important accounts and do damage.

what to do about it

Use strong and unique passwords combined with two factor authentication (2FA). That’s it. Doing so will make you significantly more secure on the internet.

What makes a password strong?

  • length (> 16 characters, ideally longer)

  • complexity

  • impersonal (no dates, initials, names, nicknames, etc)

But the greatest of these is length:

  • uP4d*83 <– cracked in minutes
  • ball coffee charter kidnap outlet <– cracked in centuries

Unique Passwords

Do NOT reuse passwords for accounts connected to:

  • email: email is the key to the castle since someone can reset nearly all other passwords if they have access to your email
  • banking: bank, paypal, taxes, investments, venmo, etc - this is your money, protect it!
  • retail: Amazon, online shopping, or anywhere where you’ve submitted and stored credit card info
  • social media: Facebook, Instagram, Twitter, LinkedIn, etc - these may seem less important, but if they’re hacked they can be used for social engineering to get access to more important accounts or for trying to scam people that trust you

Two Factor Authentication (2FA)

In addition to a password, you can add a second factor to better prove your identity when logging in. After you submit your password, you’ll be asked to submit another piece of information that only you would have. Examples, ordered from best to worst:

  • hardware security key, like yubikey (best, but most technical)
  • phone key (not SMS)
  • authenticator code
  • phone SMS code
  • email code (worst)

Configure two factor authentication on all important accounts where available and be very weary of accounts where two factor is not available.

Passwords Managers

A good password manager solves two of the three baseline practices: strong and unique passwords.

  • it lives in your browser and on your phone
  • it can generate complex passwords that are unique per website
  • it fills passwords in the browser for you
  • BUT it is the key to the castle so make sure this is very secure

I recommend 1Password, but a serious and entirely valid alternative (especially for the tech averse): pen & paper (just make sure passwords are long and unique).

Additional Practices

  • update your devices and browser regularly so that security updates are installed.
  • use a strong password / pin on your computer and phone

Read more tips at 1password.com.

Parting Words

This can all feel intimidating, but it’s worth it. You will have better peace of mind knowing that your internet identity and assets are more secure. When you get that alert at 11pm that someone is trying to reset your password on Facebook, you can ignore and rest easy knowing that they won’t be able to.
